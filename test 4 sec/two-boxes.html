The Setup

Veritasium recently published a thought-provoking piece exploring deep questions about free will, what it really means to be rational, and ultimately, how one should live life. One of the central ideas is captured in a famous thought experiment involving a supercomputer and two boxes — a scenario that challenges our intuitions about choice, prediction, and rational decision-making.

You walk into a room. There's a supercomputer and two boxes on the table. One box is open: it contains $1,000. No trick. The other is a mystery box — you cannot see inside.

You also know this supercomputer is extraordinarily good at predicting people. It has correctly predicted the choices of thousands of people facing this exact problem. It has been correct almost every time.

The computer made its prediction before you walked in. The boxes are already set. It is not trying to trick you. Its only goal is to predict correctly.

The Problem

You have two options. You can take both boxes — the mystery box plus the $1,000. Or you can take only the mystery box, leaving the thousand dollars on the table.

Here is what determines what's inside the mystery box: if the supercomputer predicted you would take only the mystery box, it placed $1,000,000 inside. If it predicted you would take both, it placed nothing.

"The supercomputer made its prediction before you knew about the problem, and it has already set up the boxes."

The prediction is already locked in. The boxes are already filled — or not. Whatever you decide right now cannot change what's inside. So what do you do?

Two Sides

This is Newcomb's Problem, first posed by physicist William Newcomb in 1960 and popularised by philosopher Robert Nozick. It has split decision theorists ever since, and no consensus exists to this day.

The case for two-boxing: The boxes are already set. Nothing you do changes what's inside the mystery box. Taking both boxes always nets you $1,000 more than taking one. Regardless of what the computer predicted, you are strictly better off taking both. This is the dominant strategy — the cornerstone of classical game theory.

The case for one-boxing: The supercomputer is nearly infallible. If you're the kind of person who takes both boxes, it predicted that — and the mystery box is empty. If you're the kind of person who takes only one, it predicted that too — and you walk away with a million dollars. Almost everyone who one-boxes leaves rich. Almost everyone who two-boxes leaves with a thousand. The evidence is overwhelming: one-boxers win.

The Payoff Matrix

Your choice Computer predicted one-box Computer predicted two-box
Take one box $1,000,000 $0
Take both boxes $1,001,000 $1,000

The two-boxer sees a matrix where taking both always adds $1,000. The one-boxer sees a world where the left column is overwhelmingly likely — and the only way to land there is to be a one-boxer.

Both arguments are logically coherent. That's what makes the problem so durable. It doesn't reveal a gap in your knowledge. It reveals a gap in the foundations of rationality itself.

What It Really Asks

Newcomb's Problem is, at its core, a question about what kind of agent you are. Not just what you will do — but what the universe can reliably predict about you. If your choices are sufficiently predictable, then "rational" one-boxing produces better outcomes than "rational" two-boxing. Your decision theory needs to account for the fact that you are the kind of entity whose decision algorithm can be modelled and anticipated.

Veritasium's framing pushes the thought experiment toward its natural endpoint: if determinism is true and the supercomputer has a perfect model of your brain, is there even a meaningful sense in which you are choosing? Or are you simply the execution of a prediction already made?

That's the real box. And it was sealed before you walked in.
